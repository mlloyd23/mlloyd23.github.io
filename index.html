Using genomic tools to understand the evolutionary
responses of hosts and diseases to the rapidly changing environment

As a member of the Pespeni Lab, I'm interested in understanding how species populations will persist through future anthropogenic environmental changes. Specifically, I want to understand how host/disease dynamics will evolve through these changes. The system we use to explore our questions are echinoderms and their microbiome and virome in the rocky intertidal marine ecosystem.

Sea Star Wasting

How and why do large epidemics happen?

"Sea star wasting" refers to a wildlife disease epidemic event in sea stars on the West Coast of North America (summer 2013-present). Likely the largest and most dramatic wildlife disease epidemic event recorded, sea star wasting provides an important and unique opportunity to study disease dynamics through an extremely virulent disease epidemic.

Evolution to Rapid Environmental Change

Can species evolve fast enough to persist in a rapidly changing environment?

Sea urchin larvae are highly sensitive to differences in temperature and ocean chemistry and provide insight to the impacts of anthropogenic environmental change such as increased ocean temperature and acidity. Results from our work indicate that, selection acts on purple urchin larvae kept in conditions of varying acidity and those kept in more acidic water suffer loses in genetic diversity. We want to expand these results to understand how temperature and disease will play a role in a multiple stressor experiment.
